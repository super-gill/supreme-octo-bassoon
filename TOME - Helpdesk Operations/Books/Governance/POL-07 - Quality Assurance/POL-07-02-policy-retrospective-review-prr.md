## 7.2.2 Purpose

To provide a structured, blameless quality control review following the handling of incidents, with the aim of identifying whether process or policy failed, and feeding improvements back into the system. The PRR is a quality control instrument, its purpose is to audit whether the Helpdesk's own procedures held up under real conditions, not to assess the conduct of individuals.

PIRs are developmental in nature and are explicitly separate from any disciplinary assessment.

## 7.2.3 Scope

This policy applies to all team members involved in the handling of a major operational or major security incident, and to any incident where a PRR is required by another policy in this manual, including the Escalation Policy, Critical Incident Policy, Major Operational Incident Policy, and Major Security Incident Policy. PIRs are facilitated by the Team Leader or Helpdesk Manager.

## 7.2.4 Supporting Documents

All supporting documents can be found in Nexus.

The PRR and the After Incident Report (ESR) are separate processes with different purposes and should not be confused.

The PRR is an internal quality control process. It reviews whether Digital Origin's process and policy functioned correctly during an incident. It is never shared with the customer and its findings are not intended for use outside the Helpdesk.

The ESR is a factual record of events relating to a specific ticket or incident. It may be shared with the customer and is designed to be evidence-grade. Where both a PRR and an ESR are conducted on the same incident, they run independently and neither feeds into nor replaces the other.

## 7.2.5 Core Principles

The PRR exists to find and fix process failures, not to find and blame people. If a person's actions are identified as the root cause of an incident, rather than a gap in process or policy, the PRR has nothing further to conclude with respect to that individual. It records the finding and closes. Any conduct matter is handled separately through the Disciplinary Process.

> Raising concerns or self-reporting failures during a PRR will never result in disciplinary action.

## 7.2.6 When a PRR is Required

A PRR must be conducted following:

- Any major operational incident where the Major Operational Incident Policy was invoked
- Any major security incident where the Major Security Incident Policy was invoked
- Any critical incident where the response fell below the expected standard
- Any escalation where a PRR was required by the Escalation Policy (e.g. where the catch-all escalation trigger was used)
- Any incident where a non-technical coordinator fulfilled the Workload Coordinator role
- Any other incident where the Team Leader or Helpdesk Manager determines a PRR would be beneficial

A PRR is not required for every critical incident or escalation. The decision to conduct one should be proportionate to the incident's impact and any identified process concerns.

## 7.2.7 Timing

A PRR debrief should occur within 1 to 3 business days of resolution. The meeting should not exceed 15 minutes.

## 7.2.8 Attendees

- Incident Owner
- Communicating Agent (where applicable)
- Workload Coordinator (where applicable)
- Team Leader (if not already involved in one of the above roles)
- Helpdesk Manager

## 7.2.9 Discussion Points

- Timeline summary
- What worked well at a process and tooling level, and where process, tooling, or detection fell short
- Opportunities to improve:
  - Tooling
  - Detection
  - Communication
  - Escalation
- Identification of training or process gaps
- Follow-up actions, which must be assigned and tracked to completion

## 7.2.10 Conclusion Criteria

The PRR concludes when one of the following is reached:

- A process or policy failure has been identified and a corrective action has been raised
- No process or policy failure is identified, the process held, and the outcome was the result of factors outside the scope of the PRR (including individual conduct)
- Both of the above apply to different elements of the incident

Where an individual's actions are identified as the root cause and no process gap exists, the PRR concludes at that point. It does not make disciplinary recommendations.

## 7.2.11 Document and Procedure Review

Following each PRR, the following must be reviewed and updated where findings indicate a gap:

- Affected documentation or guidelines based on findings
- Any customer-facing documentation where relevant
- The policy or procedure that triggered the PRR should be reviewed each time it is triggered

## 7.2.12 Relationship to Continual Improvement

All follow-up actions arising from a PRR are recorded in the continual improvement log and tracked to completion. Where a PRR identifies a systemic process failure, a corrective action is raised in accordance with the Nonconformity and Corrective Action policy.

Where a PRR identifies possible misconduct, it records only the minimum factual note necessary to explain the referral. Questions of intent, culpability, mitigation, and sanction are outside the PRR and are determined only through the disciplinary process.

### 7.2.12.1 How we measure compliance

PRR completion rates and the quality of documented findings and action items are reviewed periodically by the Helpdesk Manager. Outstanding follow-up actions are tracked to completion. PRR compliance is reviewed at Management Review.

### 7.2.12.2 Record keeping and documentation

PRR findings, action items, and outcomes are documented in the incident ticket and retained. Document updates resulting from PRR findings are recorded with the relevant policy or procedure. PRR records are retained indefinitely as part of QMS documentation.

### 7.2.12.3 How we address shortfall

Failure to complete a required PRR is escalated to the Helpdesk Manager. Where follow-up actions are not completed within an agreed timeframe, this is escalated to senior management.
